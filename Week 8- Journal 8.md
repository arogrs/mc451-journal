# Weekly Journal Entry Guide

## How to Approach Your Reflection

Each week, you'll reflect on course material using **one of three approaches**. Pick the one that connects most to what you're thinking about this week.

---

## The Three Approaches

### 🔗 The Connector
**Definition:** Link this week's material to something bigger—your project, a previous concept, real-world examples, or your own experience.

**Example Prompt:** "How does this week's reading connect to what I'm working on?"

---

### 🔧 The Troubleshooter
**Definition:** Document a challenge you faced and how you worked through it—technical, conceptual, or practical.

**Example Prompt:** "What was difficult this week, and how did I solve it?"

---

### 🎯 The Critic
**Definition:** Ask a critical question about the material. Challenge an assumption, identify a gap, or explore an implication.

**Example Prompt:** "What question or assumption from this week intrigues or concerns me?"

---

## Requirements

- **Length:** 250-300 words
- **Due:** Every Friday by 11:59 PM
- **Choose ONE approach** and delete the other two
- **Tag:** Include `#journal #weekX` at the bottom

# Week X: [Journal 8]

**Date:** [March 25]
**Phase:** [Journalist / Architect / Translator / Analyst / Publisher]
**Approach Used:** [Connector]

## Your Reflection

[I've gotten so used to jumping straight into labeling things or finding patterns as quickly as possible. This chapter made it clear that doing that too early can actually lead to misunderstanding the data, especially when dealing with something as complex as music and lyrics. Something that stood out to me what the idea that you need to sit with the data before trying to measure it. In my own work, I rush int creating categories or trying to get answers, but this reading showed me that without immersion, those categories might not reflect what's happening. For example, the idea that lyrics can have one meaning on paper and a completely different meaning when combined with the music made me realize how easy it is to misunderstand things if you only look at its surface level. This chapter also connected to chapter 7 that talked about research questions. Chapter 7 was about asking the right questions and chapter 8 is about understanding the data needed to answer that questions. It feels like this two almost depend on each other. Even if I have a strong research question, I won't be able to answer it well unless I understand the context and patterns within my first dataset. I don't usually take notes but I can see how it would help me keep track of my thinking and notice patterns over time. Instead of trying to remember everything, writing memos would help me build a clearer understanding of what I'm seeing.]
