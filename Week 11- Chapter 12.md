# Weekly Journal Entry Guide

## How to Approach Your Reflection

Each week, you'll reflect on course material using **one of three approaches**. Pick the one that connects most to what you're thinking about this week.

---

## The Three Approaches

### 🔗 The Connector
**Definition:** Link this week's material to something bigger—your project, a previous concept, real-world examples, or your own experience.

**Example Prompt:** "How does this week's reading connect to what I'm working on?"


---

## Requirements

- **Length:** 250-300 words
- **Due:** Every Friday by 11:59 PM
- **Choose ONE approach** and delete the other two
- **Tag:** Include `#journal #weekX` at the bottom

## Your Reflection

[This chapter showed me that collecting and coding data is not the final step. There is still a lot of worked needed to be done before actual analysis can begin. I did originally think that once the coding process was finished, the data would be ready to use. I now can see that real data is often messy and needs to be cleaned and organized before it can actually be analyzed properly. I've been focused on creating reliable and consistent data but this chapter forces me to also focus on preparing the data for analysis. Even with the coding being done correctly, there's still a possibility that small issues like inconsistent labels, missing values, or duplicate entires can cause problems for me later. For example, if one song is labeled positive and another is labeled Positive, the computer will treat them as different categories. This shows why it is important to standardize everything. I found the idea of reproducibility important. Using a tool like R instead of something like Excel makes sure that every step is recorded and can be repeated. This connects to real life where people need to show exactly how they got their results. If everything is done by clicking around in a spreadsheet, it would be hard to explain or repeat those steps. A script helps make the process clear. Organizing messy data is similar to organizing something like files or schedules. If things aren't structured properly, it becomes harder to find patters or make decisions. Data analysis is not just about running tests. It starts with cleaning and organizing data in a careful way so that my results are accurate and trustworthy.]
